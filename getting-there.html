Practical Guide

Getting There

Four hours of good highway separate Panama City from the peninsula — and another world of gravel roads separates the coast from the highlands. Here's how to do both.

Home › Getting There

The Main Route

Panama City to Chitré

By car: take the Pan-American Highway (the Interamericana) west across the Bridge of the Americas or the Centennial Bridge, past Penonomé and Aguadulce, to the crossroads at Divisa (about 3 hours). Turn south onto the National Highway toward the peninsula; Chitré is roughly 40 minutes further, via Parita. The whole run is around 250 km — figure 3.5 to 4 hours with a stop. Roads are paved and generally good; watch for slow trucks and sudden rain.

By bus: comfortable coaches leave the Albrook Grand Terminal in Panama City for Chitré throughout the day, taking about 4 hours for a modest fare. From Chitré's regional terminal, minibuses fan out to every district town — Ocú, Pesé, Las Minas, Los Pozos — as well as south to Las Tablas and Pedasí.

By air: Chitré has a small airport with limited domestic service that comes and goes with the seasons; check current schedules. Most travelers simply drive or ride the bus.

Into the highlands

The hill country of western Herrera — including Loma Ranchito — is reached by secondary roads that climb from the plain through Ocú, Las Minas, or Los Pozos. Pavement gives way to gravel, grades get honest, and in the rainy season a high-clearance vehicle (ideally 4x4) is the difference between a scenic drive and a long afternoon. Local minibuses and shared pickups serve the district towns on regular runs and the outlying communities less frequently — ask at the Chitré terminal for the day's schedule, and expect Spanish.

Rule of thumb: anything on the plain you can reach in a rental sedan. For the highlands in the green season — or anywhere past the end of the pavement — go 4x4, leave early, and fuel up in Chitré or Ocú first.

When to come

The dry season (mid-December to April) is the classic choice: reliable roads, blue skies, and the heart of the festival calendar, including Carnaval. The green season (May to November) means afternoon downpours and slick gravel, but also emerald hills, full rivers, whale season on the coast, and towns without a single other visitor. The heat is real year-round on the plain — the highlands are the built-in escape.

Practical notes

  • Money: Panama uses the US dollar. ATMs exist in Chitré, Las Tablas, and larger towns — carry cash for villages, fondas, and festivals.
  • Language: Spanish everywhere; English is scarce outside hotels in Pedasí. Even a little Spanish transforms the trip.
  • Phone signal: good in towns and on main roads, patchy in the hills. Download offline maps before climbing.
  • Where to stay: Chitré has the widest range of hotels and makes the best base; Las Tablas and Pedasí have good options; the highlands have essentially none — visit them as day trips.
  • Driving culture: defensive and unhurried wins. Horns are greetings, cattle have right of way, and every small town has speed bumps (muertos) — trust that they're there before you see them.
  • What to pack: sun protection, a light rain layer, footwear that can handle mud, binoculars for the bird-rich coast, and one decent outfit — Azuero dresses up for its fiestas.
