The Festival Year

In Azuero the calendar isn't measured in months — it's measured in fiestas. Here's how the region celebrates, from Carnaval's water trucks to September's mejorana guitars.

Movable feasts (Carnaval, Semana Santa, Corpus Christi) shift each year with the church calendar — always confirm dates before booking.

The Big Four

Festivals worth planning a trip around

Carnaval de Las Tablas — February or March

Panama's Carnaval is biggest not in the capital but in a town of some ten thousand people. Las Tablas splits into two rival factions — Calle Arriba and Calle Abajo — each with its own queen, floats, musicians, and fireworks budget, locked in a century-old duel of extravagance. Days belong to the culecos, tanker trucks that hose down dancing crowds in the tropical heat; nights belong to the parades and the pyrotechnic one-upmanship. It is loud, wet, crowded, and unforgettable. Book lodging months ahead or day-trip from Chitré.

Corpus Christi in La Villa de Los Santos — May or June

Forty days after Easter, La Villa stages one of the oldest festival traditions in the Americas: the dance of the diablicos sucios — "dirty little devils" in striped red-and-black suits and elaborately grotesque papier-mâché masks — locked in choreographed battle with the forces of good. The masks alone, many carved and painted by artisans in nearby Parita, are worth the trip; the mix of Catholic pageant and older, stranger theater is unlike anything else in Panama.

Festival del Manito — Ocú, August

Ocú's great gathering is the interior's most heartfelt festival: a celebration of campesino life featuring the matrimonio campesino (a traditional country wedding staged in full costume), the junta de embarra (a communal mud-walling of a house, done with music, seco, and hundreds of hands), and the duelo del tamarindo, a stylized machete duel. If you want to understand the culture that highland communities like Loma Ranchito belong to, this is the festival to see.

Festival Nacional de la Mejorana — Guararé, September

Founded in 1949, the Mejorana is Panama's national folklore stage. For about a week, tiny Guararé hosts delegations from every province: mejorana guitarists and décima singers in competition, tamborito drums, ox-cart parades, bull-ring events, and hundreds of polleras. It's the most complete single showcase of Panamanian tradition — and the best place to hear the five-string mejorana guitar that gives the festival its name.

Festival survival tips: bring cash (ATMs run dry), book rooms far ahead or stay in Chitré and commute, expect road closures in town centers, and treat the afternoon heat with respect — locals schedule the serious dancing for after dark.

Beyond the Headliners

Patron saints and small-town fiestas

Every town and rural community in the region — including the smallest highland settlements — has a patron saint and an annual fiesta to match: a novena of evening prayers, a procession, then music, food stalls, raffles, and dancing that runs late. These local festivals rarely make any tourism calendar, which is precisely their charm; stumble into one and you'll be handed a plate of sancocho before you can introduce yourself. Chitré's own San Juan Bautista celebrations in late June bring cabalgatas — horse parades hundreds of riders strong — straight out of the region's cowboy soul.
