Culture & Crafts

The dress, the drum, the verse, and the clay — Azuero's traditions aren't museum pieces. They're worn, danced, sung, and fired in kilns every week of the year.

The National Dress

La Pollera

Panama's national dress was perfected in Azuero, and the region guards that legacy jealously. A gala pollera is an heirloom project: a full skirt and blouse of fine white cloth covered in hand embroidery, often taking a skilled artisan a year or more of work, worn with layers of gold jewelry — chains, combs, and the trembling hairpieces called tembleques. Las Tablas and Guararé are famed for embroidery workshops; a finished gala pollera can cost as much as a car, and families commission them for daughters years in advance.

Herrera has its own claim: the pollera ocueña of Ocú, plainer and older in style — white with distinctive dark stitching, worn with the region's woven sombrero blanco ocueño. Its unshowy elegance says a lot about the highland character of western Herrera, where the everyday montuno (a man's embroidered shirt and cutoff trousers) began as literal field clothing.

The single best day to see polleras en masse is July 22, during the Festival de Santa Librada in Las Tablas — see the festival calendar.

Music: drum, guitar, and voice

Three sounds define the interior. The tamborito — "little drum" — is Panama's national dance: a circle of clapping singers led by a cantalante, drummers hammering out the pulse, and couples taking turns in the center in a stylized courtship. The mejorana is a small five-string guitar, born on the peninsula, whose repetitive, hypnotic figures accompany country dances and the sung poetry of the décima — ten-line improvised verses in a form inherited from Spain's Golden Age, still performed (and fiercely judged) at festivals like Guararé's. Listen too for the saloma, the yodeling cry that riders and farmers pitch across valleys — half song, half greeting, all Azuero.

The artisans

  • La Arena pottery — Herrera's craft capital turns local clay into water jars, cookpots, and painted decorative ware, using techniques with pre-Columbian roots. You can watch potters at work in roadside workshops and buy straight from the kiln.
  • Devil masks of Parita — the fantastically horned, fanged papier-mâché masks worn by Corpus Christi's diablicos are an art form of their own, and Parita's mask-makers are the acknowledged masters.
  • Ocú's weavers and embroiderers — source of the pollera ocueña and the white ocueño hat, plaited from junco fibers.
  • Saddlers, leatherworkers, and cart-builders — cattle country keeps practical crafts alive; a hand-tooled saddle or a painted ox-cart wheel is as authentic an Azuero artifact as any pollera.

The cooperative spirit

Perhaps the region's deepest tradition isn't an object at all. The junta — the gathering of neighbors to do one family's big job together — survives throughout the interior, most famously as the junta de embarra: raising the mud-and-cane walls of a house in a single day of coordinated, musical, seco-fueled communal labor. Ocú stages one each August at the Festival del Manito, but in highland communities like Loma Ranchito the everyday versions — harvest juntas, aqueduct repairs, festival preparations — are simply how things get done.

Buying crafts: buy directly from workshops where possible — La Arena for pottery, Ocú and Las Tablas for textiles, Parita for masks. Prices are fair, bargaining is gentle, and the money goes straight to the maker.
