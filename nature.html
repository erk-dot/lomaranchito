The Outdoors

Nature & Wild Places

Azuero is Panama's most farmed corner — which makes its surviving wild places all the more striking: cloud-touched ridges, a salt-crusted badland, and mudflats that feed ten thousand shorebirds.

The Green Roof

El Montuoso Forest Reserve

On Herrera's western edge, the Reserva Forestal El Montuoso protects roughly 10,000 hectares of the peninsula's last significant highland forest — the "water factory" of the region. The La Villa River, which supplies Chitré and the coastal towns, is born on these ridges, and the contrast is unmissable: below, dry cattle plain; up here, moss, bromeliads, and morning cloud snagged in the canopy.

The reserve sits in the Las Minas district, in the same band of hills as Loma Ranchito — this is the landscape that gives the community its springs, cool nights, and hummingbirds. Trails are informal and local guidance is essential; arrange visits through the district towns, and treat any hike here as a proper backcountry outing.

Sarigua: the "desert" that isn't

Just past Parita, Sarigua National Park protects one of Panama's strangest landscapes: a cracked, salt-crusted badland of red and white earth where temperatures soar and almost nothing grows. It's often called a desert, but it's really an albina — a coastal zone stripped by centuries of deforestation and salt intrusion, a dramatic warning about land use on the dry arc of the Pacific coast. It is also one of Panama's most important archaeological zones: pre-Columbian fishing settlements here date back as much as 11,000 years. Come early in the morning, bring water, and expect scenery closer to Mars than to the tropics.

Shorebirds at Playa El Agallito

Chitré's beach is a mudflat — and that's the point. When the tide retreats at Playa El Agallito it exposes kilometers of invertebrate-rich flats that draw huge concentrations of migratory shorebirds: sandpipers, plovers, whimbrels, and more, best from roughly September through April. Local biologists have studied and banded birds here for decades. Bring binoculars, check the tide table, and walk out as the water falls.

Isla Iguana

Off Pedasí at the peninsula's tip, the Refugio de Vida Silvestre Isla Iguana is a small island ringed by some of the Gulf of Panama's best-preserved coral: white sand, green iguanas, one of the country's largest frigatebird colonies, and good snorkeling on calm days. Boats run from Playa El Arenal in Pedasí (about 20–30 minutes); pay the park fee, pack out everything, and go on a weekday if you can.

Whales, turtles, and the wild southwest

From about July to October, humpback whales calve in the warm waters off the peninsula — boat trips from Pedasí regularly encounter mothers with calves. Sea turtles nest on several southern beaches, and the remote Cerro Hoya National Park, on the peninsula's rugged southwestern corner, guards the last big block of old-growth forest in the region — reachable only by 4x4 or boat, and worth it for the truly determined.

Season notes: Dry season (Dec–Apr) means dusty roads and easy access but browner landscapes. The rainy months turn the hills green and the rivers full — and highland gravel roads slick. Whale season peaks Jul–Oct; shorebird numbers peak in the northern winter.
